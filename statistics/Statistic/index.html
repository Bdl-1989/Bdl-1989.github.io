    Statistic

    Posted on 2020-12-06 Edited on 2020-12-08 In statistics Views:

      learning notes。

    Notes

    • descriptive statistics usually include things like where the middle of the data is - what statisticians call measures of central tendency - and measures of how spread out the data are.

    • we ask inferential statistics to do all sorts of much more complicated work for us. inferential statistics lets us test an idea or hypothesis

    lesson 3

    • Mathematical thinking is about seeing the world in a different way. Whhich means sometimes seeting beyond our intuition or gut feeling.

    lesson 4

    • Normal: A distribution of data that has roughly the same amount of data on either side of the middle, and has its most common values around the middle of the data.

    • Distribution: shows us how often each values occurs in out data set

    • Median: the middle number if we lined up our data from smallest to largest

    The difference of median and average

    • The word MODE comes from the latin word MODUS, which means ‘manner, fashion, or style’ and gives us the french expression ‘a la mode’, meaning fashionable

      Mode: the value that appears most in our data set

    • The fact that the median and mean are the same tells us that the distribution is symmetric: that there’s equal amount of data on either side of the median, and equal amounts on either side of the mean.

    lesson 5

    • Range takes the largest number in our data set and subtracts the smallest number in the set to give us the distance between these two extremes. the larger the distance, the more spread out our data is.

    • Interquartile range: Doesn’t consider extreme values; The IQR looks at the spread of the middle 50% of your data.

    • But it turns out that if you use this same formula to calculate the variance of a sample, it would be ‘biased’. That is, the sample variance would consistently be a little smaller than the real variance of the popluation.

    We divide by the number of samples minus 1 in order to get the sample variance to be unbiased - or a better guess for the population variance.

    • Standard deviation: The average amount we expect a point to differ (or deviate) from the mean.

    lesson 6

    • Quantitative data: Numbers that have both order and consistent spacing.

    • Categorical data: Doesn’t have a meaningful order or consistent spacing.

    LESSON 9

    • Measures the way two variables move together, both the direction and closeness of their movement.

    • To get around that, we use the standard deviations to scale our correlation so that it is always between -1 and 1. This is our correlation coefficient.

    • Squared correlation: r^2 is always between 0 and 1, and tells us - in decimal form - how much of the variance in one variable is predicted by the other.

      eg. Usage predicts 70% of the variation in how healthy our lungs are.

    r^2 = 1 means you can perfectly predict one variable from the other since 100%.

    Correlation doesn’t equal causation.

    1. A causes B
    2. B causes A
    3. Variable C causes both A and B
    4. No relationship at all.

    these call spurious correlations

    Lesson 10

    • Randomness allows us to claim that before the cappuccino makers were given out, there were no systematic dffierences between the groups.

    Lesson 11

    • There are two things that can make or break a survey: The questions, and who the researcher gives the questions to.

    Lesson 12

    • When reading a science story, it’s important to note a couple things: who wrote it, who published it, who did the science and who funded the science.

    • ONly experimental studies with randomized designs and control groups have a shot at showing evidence of causation.
